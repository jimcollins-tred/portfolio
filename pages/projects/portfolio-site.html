For my personal profile website, not only was I keen to showcase my work, I also wanted the site itself to be a demonstration of my design and coding skills.

The site was initially wire-framed and designed using UXPin, allowing me to nail the look and feel before I commenced the build. I strove for a clean yet bold look, with judiciously-used animations to bring sections to life. I developed the grid structure of the site in UXPin, allowing for a relatively painless transferral into CSS Grid.

The site began life before I learnt React. There was the obvious temptation to redevelop it, but instead I saw it as an excellent opportunity to hone my HTML, CSS and JavaScript skills without relying on frameworks.

The site is also fully responsive, displaying perfectly on everything from a wide-screen desktop to a phone.

Project / Spotlight

Intersection Observer

I initially looked into event listeners to trigger the various animations, but found them too resource-intensive. Instead I used Intersection Observer, an excellent API that enabled me to observe elements on a page and trigger animated transitions whenever they scrolled into the viewport.

Intersection Observer also allowed me to trigger the playing of videos only when they scroll into the viewport, and to pause them when they leave.

A modular approach

One of the great benefits of React, of course, is how easy it makes building modular, reusable components. To replicate this with standard HTML and JavaScript, I turned to Web Components. Rather than replicate the nav code on every page, I instead encapsulated both the HTML and JS logic inside a component that could be reused throughout the site. This was also helpful for the header, footer, hero image and ‘contact me’ elements of the site.

Additionally I used SCSS to modularise my CSS into a logical structure.

{}

Nav menu

One element I was particularly pleased with was the popup navigation menu. The background animation was achieved by displaying a zero width and height circle in the top right corner, which was then scaled to fill the screen when the menu icon was clicked. Likewise the menu itself was a zero-width div on the left-hand side of the page, transitioned to full-page width to give the appearance of it sliding into view.

The hover animation for individual menu items required the use of a background gradient clipped to the text. The background appeared pale at first, but upon hover was shifted to the right to reveal a more solid colour, providing an eye-catching effect.
